The Patron

On a failed novelist in Valparaíso, the advertising man who financed his dream, and what it takes to tell him the truth.

A version of this piece appeared in Spanish in TintaLibre magazine, issue 134 (April 2025)

Part 1

I quickly realized Laura believed in Amado's own version of himself: the dream, nurtured despite innumerable failures, that he was a great intellectual.

Their second house in Barón Hill overlooked the city of Valparaíso, a maze of alleys and looping stairways built atop the Chilean Coast Range. The city was once the Southern Cone's most prosperous trade port; lately, its relatively low living costs had turned it into a hotbed for dilettantes, poets, and would-be philosophers. By seeing Amado and Laura interact during my first visit, I got the impression she admired him for being a man of ideas, without giving much concern to the quality of those ideas.

For the first time in my career as editor, I had been paid to visit a client — Amado had covered not only my bus ticket, but also the hours spent on the meeting itself, an unheard of luxury in my line of business. An aspiring editor of high ambition but minuscule renown, I felt like I was robbing him. I still remember the taste of my self-satisfaction as I got off the rickety bus and hailed a taxi down the street.

Trying to sound legit, I had told my father I had an out-of-town meeting with a client. He worried about my prospects. I would have made a perfectly adequate lawyer, he thought, yet "for some reason" had decided to make things difficult for myself. As the cab moved up and down the knolls and mounts and hummocks and tors and all other manner of small and large bumps on the road which, however inconvenient, guarantee that there is never a dull moment in Valparaíso, I thought of what he would say if he understood how precarious my life was. When I got off the cab and Amado came out to greet me, I sighed — my small "success" wasn't fooling anyone. Gazing downward, he examined a red polo shirt three sizes too big for him, his slightly bugged-out eyes fixated on what looked like a cooking oil stain, which he fruitlessly tried to remove with his thumb. Then he looked up and smiled at me through a jungle of a beard. Though his hair was blond-ish, his eyes were the color of caramel — they were beautiful.

He lived in the streets around 2007, after he parted ways with his first wife and moved to the run-down town driven by its bohemian allure. He set up a stand in an underground fair selling stuff he recovered from garbage bins. One day, a woman erected a much cleaner and better organized stand next to his. In a few days, Laura was running both stands as Amado talked breathlessly to a few elderly clients: it was love at first sale, she told me. Soon, they opened a real newsstand downtown and moved in together. Their first home was a small apartment in the "plane" — which is how porteños derisively refer to the narrow strip of seaside road that adorns their hilly coastline — which soon proved too small. In between her shifts at the newsstand, Laura, who sported a wild mane of curly hair and matching energy, cooked us a delicious cazuela. I am pretty sure she took charge of the family business almost exclusively, leaving Amado to pore over bad translations of Hannah Arendt and Argentine crime novels and to type up his hard-left blog posts on an old computer.

In the 1990s, he had been the kind of young guy who would crash an Economics panel at the International Book Fair, line up a couple of expansive rhetorical questions, and end up in a shouting match with some childhood friend who had turned liberal. More recently, Facebook and then Twitter had become his true calling. It was in many ways what he was born to do, for he always struck first and without mercy. His feed was an endless cascade of takedowns and exposés, and he always took things so far — always to an accusation of veiled fascism, of something something Pinochet, of conspiratorial denunciation — that he almost invariably kept the last word. His seven thousand or so followers really seemed to appreciate it.

Amado's blog and social media presence had gotten him some attention in the cultural niche of Valpo. Eventually, he was offered to host a show on the municipal radio station. "People usually want to hear what I have to say," he told me a few minutes after I met him without a hint of irony.

And yet despite his occasional lack of self-awareness, he could be pleasant. He bestowed me with a sense of admiration I did not deserve — and quickly adopted me, a man thirty years younger than himself straight out of a shoddy university, as his mentor, after a short test in which he asked me my opinion about every single writer in his bookcase. At some point, he could not contain himself anymore: he had to talk to me about his patron. It was he, Rodrigo, who had made all of this possible. He paid for Amado's comfortable life and he would pay for my services as well. Rodrigo was an amazing man, and Amado could not wait for me to meet him.

After I graduated college, I spent a couple of years in a humorless depression, making two dollars a day working in the basement of my uncle's bookstore. Covered in a thick layer of dust, I ran up and down the stairs carrying seemingly endless copies of John Katzenbach's The Analyst as the smell of microwaved fusilli with marinara sauce impregnated every strand of my clothes and hair.

It was a friend, the poet and editor Julieta Marchant, who pulled me out of that sad place. She trained me to be a sort of MacGyver of book production, capable of bringing any project to a close — be it by editing, copywriting, translating, transcribing or even, if necessary, doing horrid layouts in InDesign. The small outfit she set up for us was intended to absorb all of her editorial projects that did not pass muster — the ones Julieta would never have dared publish in her two well-known imprints, Cuadro de Tiza and Bisturí 10. Those two represented everything that was cool, sophisticated, and tasteful in literary Chile. They published misfits, feminists, post-structuralists and deconstructionists, and they were worshiped by writers and intellectuals, their reputation creeping out even beyond Chile's borders. Under our editorial services company, J&P Editoras, me and Julieta took on projects purely for a (meager) profit. We edited writers no reputable publisher would dare touch; not because they had done or said something questionable, but because their writing had one small problem: it was, quite simply, pretty bad. We did not call them our writers, nor did we call ourselves their publishers. It was purely transactional. They bought specific services from us — editing a manuscript, for example — and we considered them our clients.

Even within our mercenary company, Julieta took on projects of passable reputation — which she, by the way, had secured. A literary magazine for the University of Chile. A collection of historical monographs for an NGO. Being a well-known editor, Julieta was often approached by those kinds of institutions. She was also approached by strange characters, usually via Facebook: rural poets of questionable judgment; novelists who had published a decently-reviewed book over twenty years ago and had never managed to replicate the success; armchair intellectuals convinced they had finally made the right adjustments to Marx's theory of value. Those were the kinds of writers that she saved for me — the kind that made freelance editors fly away without second thought: those that, were it not for the fact that I was desperate for any kind of work, would have found themselves confined to the awkward and dangerous underworld of self-editing and self-publishing.

I took on every job not only because I could not afford not to, but because I was convinced each project represented an opportunity, however minuscule, on the road to becoming a Great Editor. Seeing my name written in the copyright page or in the colophon, often buried under some inconspicuous title like "editing assistant," "proofreader," or "producer," had not yet lost its glamour. The title of "editor," or even better, that of "lead editor," had so far eluded me, but I knew it would come eventually. And with a huge boon: I would discover a true writer among life's rejects, pick her out of the ashes of anonymity, and present her to the world, earning in an instant the fame and recognition life so far had denied me — alongside, hopefully, a full-time job. I would never have confessed this, but I harbored my own dream of success and recognition.

(I would never allow myself to forget the story of Jorge Barón Biza, one of the greatest Argentinian writers of his generation, who had been a failed art critic and a very sad person and had had his first book rejected by every reputable publisher in Buenos Aires, and then also by all the dubious ones. He was therefore forced to self-publish his memoir El desierto y su semilla (The Desert and its Seed). The book came out with humiliating typesetting and layout mistakes that made it almost impossible to read. The work of true amateurs who had charged him an arm and a leg, the copies languished in three large boxes in his apartment until the author threw himself out of a four story window, the same method by which his mother and sister had ended their own lives a few years earlier — his father, a strange combination of millionaire, dandy, criminal, and writer of porno novels, had chosen a bullet to the head so as to maintain some level of aesthetic consistency. I must confess that, at the time, I drew from this sordid story the conclusion that, had I been an editor in the late 1990s, I might have been able to make Barón Biza famous while he was still alive to enjoy it.)

My first few gigs were not encouraging, but they taught me a lot. I remember a particularly kind lady with a very posh last name who hired me to revise her unapologetically romantic third novel (the other two remained unpublished). It was weirdly abstract and even had a faint right-wing air at times, when touching on the subject of the dictatorship, but was ultimately quite sweet and full of heartbreak. And another by a transcendental Mapuche healer, except he was not Mapuche in the slightest, but rather a well-off young guy who read a couple of Carlos Castaneda books and set off into the Chilean rainforest like it was the Amazon jungle. After a couple of days living in the wild, his protagonist was taken in by a Mapuche farmer who constantly tested him, like an indigenous Mr. Miyagi, and slowly taught him the meaning of life. Quite slowly: the novel had over eight hundred pages.

Those writers, a sort of underclass that my editor friends called "kooky" or "daft," were men and women who worked all day and then spent their afternoons and nights polishing a dream; a novel that most likely would only ever be read by one person, and only because they had paid me to do it.

When Julieta called me to introduce me to Amado's project, she was her usual self — stern and short-tempered.

"Are you interested? He talks a lot, he's a bit of an incel, quite a small character," she told me through the phone, without any introduction. "But he seems like a nice enough dude overall."

I said yes before I even knew what kind of book he was writing.

In his youth Amado had been a sort of cursed poet, an enfant terrible — perhaps more enfant than terrible — of a triumphant generation, the only one in Chile that saw commercial success with literary novels during the opening of democracy in the 1990s. The only one that briefly got paid to write feature articles and imagined our humble southern nation could host a posse of aspiring Martin Amises and Ian McEwanses with a lot more enthusiasm than talent.

Alas, it was a short-lived dream. By 2005, print runs that had started at five figures fell rapidly to four and then three. Writers stopped boasting about their advances and began to turn shy when asked by successful relatives how well they were selling. Novels simply got tired of climbing off the shelves and decided they were comfortable enough. Many of those dreamers, heartened by the promise that they might earn a living by writing, were left rolling in the dust, and Amado was one of them. He had befriended Fuguet, Gumucio, Fontaine, all writers who had come in early enough to make a career, and closed the door behind them. Now they all hated him, he said hurriedly, but he hated no one, he was incapable of hate, all of his objections to them were moral and/or political — "and by that I mean Marxist" he clarified. They had sold their souls without a moment's pause, and toured Europe raking in the cash, sure they were the harbingers of the end of history, while he was living off the streets in Valparaíso, selling useless bits out of a vaguely esoteric-looking blanket, he exhaled, and cackled.

Where as an artist Amado had failed in the conventional way — towards the ground — Rodrigo had managed to fail upwards. He studied Art History and attempted to paint for a few years, but he wasn't making much selling his paintings. When he stumbled onto advertising through an uncle's friend, the profession was just starting to take off in Chile, like so many things at the time. He was sharp; he moved quickly from intern to copywriter to manager to the right hand of the CEO of a big studio, then left to open his own. He employed a small team and worked on curated projects for big clients like Samsung and Zara. He became rich — not helipad rich, mind you, nor family office rich or anything of the sort; I mean rich in the sense that he had thoroughly ceased surviving, and from now on his life turned in search for something else to do, something unfathomable.

Even though he made it big, Rodrigo told me, he never left behind his early aspirations: art, to him, was still greater than commerce. I met him only a few months after I did Amado: he called out of the blue and asked to meet him in a fancy coffee shop in the biggest mall in Santiago, Costanera Center. He showed up exactly on time. I remember being struck by the elegance of his woven t-shirt and what looked like a tight-knit woolen suit jacket of a kind I had never seen outside of a TV screen.

It surprised me to discover his contempt for his profession: anyone can sell technology or fashion, he said. Selling culture, on the other hand, was a tall order. He was up to it, but it had to be culture of the highest quality. If it was top notch, he would make it all the rage. He then pulled out a black notebook, an expensive Leuchtturm 1917, and told me it was German and of an exceptional quality, and that notebooks of such fine taste were not made in Chile. Why? He wondered the same. But he thought it had to do with the defeatism of Chilean cultural institutions, especially the government and artists. There was a lack of impetus, he said, a constant negativity, an excessive focus on the abuses of the past.

"The dictatorship sapped Chilean culture of all its generative energy," Rodrigo said with an air of deep concern. "I have done well for myself, the future of my family is secure. So why on Earth should I not spend my money trying to help good culture be born in this country?" As a man of the Concertación, the centrist coalition that governed Chile in the 1990s, he had substituted the word art almost entirely for the much more manageable culture.

We had just met. I was a nobody in Chilean literature. Yet he said something like this:

"We can disrupt the publishing world, you and I. I can see in your eyes that you are good at what you do. Any quality project you have, we can turn into good business. I just came back from Europe and saw these stands, machines for selling books in malls and airports. Have you seen this?" he asked, pulling out his phone. "This is the future. The bookstores you see around us, they are all running a funny business. They take a huge cut of every sale and leave the writer and the publisher with nothing. We can cut back on costs and drive all these dumb motherfuckers out of business," Rodrigo told me, his eyes glinting.

I won't hide that a part of me wished to despise him. He was one of the people who, through all his corporate talk and business logic and unchecked vanity, had turned the country — so I thought at the time — into a gigantic department store. Another side of me, though, could not help but be in awe of all the tiny details that made up my impression of his wealth. He was a different kind of person from any I had known. His elegant haircut, his transparent rimmed glasses, the collar of his button-up shirt, his nails, all seemed to have come to this world finished and perfectly formed. The coffee bill he paid for that afternoon would have covered a whole week of my lunches, which usually took place in a supermarket cafeteria. He made for such a contrast to the young writers and editors I spent most of my time with: thoroughly used clothes, broken phone screens, and a backpack of resentments borne from a life-long struggle for survival — over time, said struggle tended to embitter them. Even a brief conversation with fellow writers could leave you with a sense that the world was made up only of roadblocks, injustices and impossibilities. By contrast, I found Rodrigo's presence expansive, almost limitless. And he seemed to take me seriously, just like he took Amado seriously. What if I could convince him to finance one of my own projects? I had to tread carefully.

For a while, Rodrigo had a house in Reñaca beach, quite close to Valpo, and during summer and spring drove his family back and forth from Santiago almost every weekend. It was on that tight coastal road, looking for something on the radio to take his mind off his children, that he stumbled onto Amado's voice. He instantly understood he was a cracking guy, so well-read and penetrating and full of energy. So, he shot him a message through his blog, met him for coffee once or twice, and pitched him an idea: Amado would write a novel and Rodrigo would market and sell it. That one had never written and the other never read a line of fiction in their lives was immaterial. What mattered was that Amado had the spirit and the talent and Rodrigo had the advertising genius.

There was one small problem, though. If Amado was to have time for writing, he would need to be at the newsstand less often, and abandon some other odd jobs and commitments. As was his custom, Rodrigo landed on a solution exactly as the issue threatened to ruin his plans. He would pay Amado a good monthly wage for as long as he needed to write his masterpiece. And because he could, he did. At his house in Barón Hill, Amado proudly showed me a folder where he kept every invoice he had made out to Rodrigo's company. It dawned on me that, ever since I had published my first novel a few years prior, I hadn't received a single royalty statement, invoice, check, or even e-mail of any kind from my publisher.

Once his draft was ready after two years of heavy writing, Amado needed an editor, a pair of eyes to help him see his work from the outside. Rodrigo, as he told me at the coffee shop, was curious about the book: was it good? And how much work did it need, in my opinion? He wouldn't read it himself yet, of course; he didn't want to intervene in the creative process. He also was looking for someone from the outside who could look in. Someone who could tell him whether the quality stuff that would allow him to change the Chilean cultural scene forever had finally materialized.

Caught unawares as always, I became that person.

Part 2

"They are years, seconds, life that the eye's pupil sucks like he who sucks the bone marrow of a dead cow. And here you, him, her, me, that same trembling marrow in the air of a street that screams like a hyena and laughs cowering above the human condition, gifted to us by a one-eyed god. A crazy seesaw, sounds of an orchestra of mournful muscularity, cotton to the air, a caterpillar that will never have wings, a fish that looks askance at the lake, laughing the last laugh."

This is how Amado's masterpiece began. As often happens in my line of business, during our first phone call I had to break his heart. He had an intuition, like many writers do after finishing a work they care about, that he might have finally done it. Amado had spent tortuous months trying to instantiate the poetic ideal, fighting with images and words, devoting hundreds of sleepless hours at his desk, wondering if he'd make it out alive. And he was proud of his results. He had every right to be. Like a rocket pilot sent to pick up an astronaut from the International Space Station, my first job was to bring him back to disappointing Earth.

I must say, he took it better than I expected. He resisted, sure, and made excuses, and tried to argue his way out of it several times. But he also paid me a respect I had not earned. Even though some part of him wished to wiggle himself out, discard my comments, and keep his self-image as a consummate virtuoso, he clenched his jaw and endured. Some other, better part of himself recognized I was not lying. And he agreed to set out with me in an awful process, that of vivisecting his loving creation like a thing, and putting it back together with some sort of strategy in mind.

What followed were some of the happiest months of my life. Like Amado, Rodrigo put me on his company's payroll at a reasonable fee for every month of work we took on — unlike most jobs, for which I was paid a flat sum. It was enough at the time for me to not have to worry about basic food or shelter, and the process gave me less than a full month of work given I had to wait for weeks at a time for Amado's revisions. This meant that for the first time in years I had time to wander about and look at the ceiling and pretend to write in between our intense editing sessions. We worked deliberately and held long conversations that resembled rambling classes, with a bit of literary theory, history, and practical writerly advice I made up on the spot. No matter how scattershot, my ramblings always felt relevant because Amado had so much to catch up on. I remember spending hours trying to explain to him his female characters had to be more than just annoying, vindictive, or demeaning, and I saw his mind almost crack under the cognitive effort he exerted. He developed a habit of calling me, like my mother, on the strangest of occasions, and would pepper me with anxious messages if I did not pick up on the spot. I remember the perplexity on my girlfriend's face as she asked me what was going on when my phone would not stop vibrating, and acting out annoyance at Amado's relentlessness; but the truth is I found it exhilarating, the idea that someone cared so much about my opinions. I later scolded him during our phone calls like an aunt would her favorite nephew.

Painstakingly, Amado learned to write slowly and humbly instead of quickly and proudly, steadily internalizing that there was no path to improving that did not involve looking honestly at the crap that initially came out of us, deconstructing it, and meticulously turning it back into the truth we had glimpsed at while writing. This unpleasant process could even become fun if one turned less sensitive to the constant pulsations of vanity, which wrestled always to be heard and be the only thing that was heard.

We worked hard, and in about six months whipped his manuscript up into shape. The result was not perfect. Amado still had an unusual mind and all too common ideas. His subject was not the richest: the aftermath of the dictatorship, the humiliation of the most overrepresented generation in Chilean literary history, and a portrait of people who decided to blend into neoliberalism and fascism because they were not brave or creative enough to resist it. Sometimes it felt less like a novel and more like a veiled political treatise. But I wouldn't want to undersell the achievement. It was a book. That could be read. By other people. And you may not believe me, especially if you have not tried it for yourself, but this is something many writers toiling every day on our planet try and fail all their lives to achieve.

During family reunions, stories of my work with Amado would send Dad's anxiety spiraling. Why do you spend so much time on his writing? Why care? He thought, as always, that I was wasting my time: how could my relationship to this secondary character advantage me in any way? Why waste so much time on him? Surely I should be pitching ideas to Rodrigo: that was where the true opportunities were. Amado, on the other hand, warranted little more than doing the bare minimum and pocketing the money.

I didn't react in any way when he said these things. Not beyond smiling ironically. I knew he had the best intentions. He loved me, I repeated to myself. He respected me. Paradoxically, what vexed him was that he couldn't understand why I made so many life decisions he couldn't understand. Perhaps I should've said this: "It's all your fault, Dad. Don't you remember loco Díaz?"

His pleasure at the arrival of a guest is an indelible childhood recollection. "I invited loco Díaz to lunch," he would tell me, rubbing his hands in excitement, as soon as I got into his car with my backpack hanging uncomfortably from one shoulder, full of every little thing I thought I would need for the weekend.

Dad lived in the next city over from Valparaíso, Viña del Mar, and we would drive to the coast listening to old soul songs or the soundtrack of Les Misérables, and that stupid song about "dreaming a dream" — as if dreaming something else at all was possible — stuck with me forever.

Before lunch, he would expound in detail on everything that made loco Díaz so special. A classic Valparaíso character, he loved Greek and particularly pre-Socratic philosophy and decided to truly live by its principles, which meant he had to be willing to take any conflict to its ultimate consequence, with the goal of achieving not only peace, but honor and gallantry in the wider society. "He believes that if we were all willing to kill for the slightest offence," my dad would say, if one were reckless enough to ask him, "we would all be a lot more careful about what we did and what we said to others." In practice, this meant Díaz challenged anyone who slighted him to a duel, and would constantly get into brawls around the port with men twenty years younger than himself. He often lost, even though he applied the techniques of ancient Greek boxing. The first thing he would do when he arrived, Dad said, was showcase his latest bruise to the entire table with philosophical enthusiasm.

As he told us all this, Dad was in his apex. Besides trials and laws and regulations and the history of the Soviet Union, he seemingly could think of nothing else besides funny ways to share those kinds of stories. I remember his friends as a personal collection that he kept; averse to every kind of clutter, having his pantry and his closet stocked only with the bare essentials, Dad instead collected what he called his "characters."

Inversely, he hated nothing more than formal dinners, trite conversations about everyday matters, or being forced to spend time with what he called "solemn fools," following a formulation by the poet Nicanor Parra:

For half a century
Poetry was the paradise
Of the solemn fool.
Until I came along
And built my rollercoaster.
Go up, if you feel like it.
It's not my fault if you come down
Bleeding from your nose and mouth.

As he spoke about someone like loco Díaz, I would struggle to imagine what they looked like. I expected some incredible-looking character to step through the door, perhaps carrying a live raven. Instead, I saw the silhouette of a bent-over oldish man — he looked like a beat-down, provincial university professor. Which is exactly what he was, what most of my dad's friends were. And then he and my father would talk through lunch and the whole afternoon so breathlessly, citing all kinds of names and places and historical events I could barely register, that when my friend Esteban from two floors down knocked on the door to ask if I was available to go play Super Nintendo at his apartment, I would brim with relief and brotherly love for him.

James Joyce's daughter Lucia struggled with mental health, so he took her to see an eminence: Carl Jung. Jung, who thought a lot of modernist art was "a pathological product," believed both father and daughter to be schizophrenic. "But where you manage to swim, she drowns," Jung is said to have told Joyce.

Same with you and I, Dad. Where you managed to swim, I drowned. Kooky friends were your hobby, a harmless diversion. I, always slightly misaligned, miscalibrated, on the verge but never quite living up to that concept so dear to you — Reason — tried to turn them into my profession.

Amado sent me the final version of his manuscript on July of 2019, even though it feels like over a decade has passed since. In the ensuing e-mail chain, he shared his choice of epigraph, by Anne Sexton:

"My body is useless.
It lies, curled like a dog on the carpet.
It has given up."

A few weeks of total silence passed before Rodrigo summoned me to his office. I knew he would ask about Amado's novel, but we also had something else to discuss. A few weeks prior, I had finally mustered courage enough to send him the editorial plan and budget for a project I wanted him to finance: me and Julieta's literary magazine, which I thought destined to fix the ailing Chilean editorial landscape once and for all. Qué Pasa, the last Chilean commercial literary magazine, had gone under the previous year. Only a few magazines survived, all of them tied to universities, most of them online only and paying between a hundred and zero bucks per poorly-edited article. We wanted to bring back something in print, as serious and prestigious as Julieta's publishers, modeled on the American magazines I was starting to become familiar with: non-fiction features, pitching, paid articles, the whole thing. The proposal we drafted stated the following objectives:

  1. Be impossible to ignore. Without being strident, our product cannot leave our audience indifferent. An issue of the magazine left on a coffee table must become a topic of conversation.
  2. Be a model. If we do our work exceptionally well, our magazine should become a model for the future. We must be capable of placing topics, questions, or people on the cultural map.
  3. Be ethical. We pay what the commissioned work is worth. We want our collaborators to have a dignified life. We oppose the injustices that plague our continent. We are openly environmentalist and feminist. Our project is an invitation to a way of seeing the world.
  4. Be extremely demanding. We will not publish an article that isn't finished. We will work side by side with writers until what they want to say is effectively captured on the page. What appears in print in our magazine will be immortalized in its best version.
  5. Be a contribution to the medium. We want to raise the standard and keep it high. When a reader opens an issue of the magazine, we want what they read to both entertain and edify them, and for them to return to their everyday life a little wiser.

And on and on it went. When I had discussed the idea with Rodrigo, he had encouraged me to be ambitious and put quality first. We budgeted collaborators' fees, print costs, wages, a modest office space, and came to an annual budget of a bit under thirteen thousand dollars a year at one issue per trimester. We titled our project The Pacific, an ironic inversion of The Atlantic, a magazine we admired and despised in equal measure.

But Rodrigo had not gotten back to us about the pitch.

His office wasn't in Sanhattan, the Santiago neighborhood most in vogue amongst corporate types. It was located in the hipster enclave of La Reina, partially hidden from view by a skillful landscape artist. The office itself was so transparent that, from the entrance, I could take in the whole view completely. I had to wait for a receptionist to open the glass door for me. Rodrigo shook my hand and led me to a conference room which had a gigantic Samsung panel mounted on the wall.

"They're our clients," he said. "They always send us their latest model."

He was distracted. Or maybe tired. His conversation was somewhat incoherent, as if he couldn't focus on any single topic fully. As small talk, he spoke about his wealthy clients and how a new generation of inheritors were trying to make Chilean capitalism more humane. He knew some of them well. They wanted to protect the environment and were totally different from their retrograde parents. He then moved on to Amado's novel. He was proud of us for having finished it. He lowered his voice.

"How is it? What do you think about it?"

It occurred to me then that perhaps he wasn't distracted. He was nervous. Expectant. I hesitated. I wanted — I think I wanted (I wanted to want) — to be truthful.

"I think it is a good first novel," I said.

"You think so? Do you think it can disrupt the literary world? Is it the next One Hundred Years of Solitude?"

Up until then, I had considered Rodrigo a relatively reasonable man. That comment sent me for a spin. I had to think a while before saying something.

"Publishing is very complicated and difficult and there are many people trying to change it and it has changed a little bit," I said cautiously. "But no one person, certainly not someone with no experience in the field, could utterly transform it with a single book. I include myself in that," I added quickly, concerned my words betrayed some irritation.

"This is a solid book to launch a new publisher on," I continued. "A new, promising writer. If he keeps at it, he may write something great one day."

"But let me ask you honestly," he said, looking me in the eye for what felt like the first time. "How good is the book? Obviously, this conversation will not leave this room. I just need to know: is it important? Is it unprecedented?"

It was only then I knew for a fact he had not read the original draft, and had probably never glanced at a single word Amado had written, and most likely had not even read a single novel for the past twenty years. I deflected: I said there were readers who would certainly care about Amado's story, and that it was an interesting contribution to the thoughts his generation had about the dictatorship and the following two decades. As I had said, it was a solid first book that would get some positive reviews, and that if Amado kept at it maybe he could come up with something truly special, in time.

"I see," Rodrigo said, settling down, breathing quietly. "That's quite interesting." And he turned to himself utterly.

Changing the subject, he made some idle conversation, got up and said he had a meeting booked in ten minutes. None of us ever brought up The Pacific. And I never heard from or saw him again.

When Amado called me, he was breathing heavily. He was angry but did not know where to put his anger.

"What did you do?" he snarled. "What did you guys talk about?"

"What?"

"You meet Rodrigo, a few days later he calls me and tells me he wants to 'reconsider our arrangement.' Are you fucking kidding me? 'Our arrangement'? We're not mobsters. He wants to cut me off!"

"Shit," I said. "Shit, Amado, I'm sorry, I didn't think he would…"

I was toast. I had apologized and so was guilty. I saw in my mind the contortions in his face: the disgust, the disappointment. We might as well had hung up then and there.

"I thought I had stood up for you," I said, "but also for myself."

Amado thought I was an idiot if I truly had not realized how much this would mess him up. And since in his eyes I was not an idiot, I had to have done it on purpose.

"He asks you something like that, you tell him: Everything's fucking great. We're dandy, we've written your masterpiece, you capitalist fuck. The book's so good it's going to make alien billionaires come down from their spaceships and fuck your brains out. Then you shut up and mind your own fucking business."

I could put up with his language and his Tarantino-esque tone, but not with what came next. He began to suggest I had done everything on purpose. My objective was to steal his patron: to get Rodrigo to pay my bills instead of his. I had probably asked him, he said, to finance my second novel: that's why I had told him Amado's work was a waste of time.

To be honest, it's not like the thought had not crossed my mind. I had considered it briefly and then discarded it, like most of the evil ideas that pass through my brain on any given day. But I had worked on the magazine pitch. Amado's accusation got to me. I was offended, hurt in my vanity, and felt my pride swell up too quickly. But I did have a split second to consider what I would do next and did not stop.

"Oh fuck you, you piece of shit, you dumb motherfucker, you intolerable, arrogant, sexist, small-minded literary fanboy, you nobody, you spiritual and creative leech: I wrote your shitty novel, and Rodrigo paid for it, and now you're all alone again, like you always have been."

It took me a ludicrous amount of effort to keep myself from saying those words. I was so fed up with Amado that I almost wanted to break up with him: to tell them we shouldn't see each other anymore. This had all been a terrible idea. I would never be a good editor or a good writer. Trying to help someone like him was as hopeless as trying to help myself. We were all dreamers. We were all fools. We were all vain. And we were all trapped.

So I just stood there in silence. A few long seconds passed.

His voice was breaking when he spoke again. He apologized so simply and earnestly that it took me a while to notice he was crying. I thought of Dad, of how he had cried once, only once, in my presence. Earnestly, deeply. Vulnerably. "They both sound the same," I thought, and then realized I had said it out loud, although luckily Amado had not heard me.

We had a long conversation then, an honest one. I told him that I was done and we would never work together again and I had to grow up and move on from my stupid fantasies. He told me he didn't know what he would do with his life from now on.

"It was too good to be true, having a patron," Amado said. "I thought for once in my life I could receive something, a gift, that had no strings attached. But there are always strings attached, Cristóbal. You should remember that."

I often think about Amado while walking around my neighborhood. I can almost see myself on the opposite sidewalk, a smartphone pressed hard against my ear, pacing and practically screaming as we discussed his book for hours on end. Was I happy? I do get a pleasant feeling in my stomach. It quickly fades when I remember our last call.

The story is a sort of shorthand for an important period of my life, one during which everything I did had a unique flair, a secret spice. I was available, open for life to come at me, willing to get into unexpected situations because I believed nothing in the world was beneath me. That magical period didn't last: it took me only a couple of months after my fallout with Amado to recognize I would never be able to rent my own apartment as an editor. Tired of living with my mother, worn out by my dad's well-meaning questions and suggestions, I left my dream of becoming an editor behind. I learned how to use LinkedIn and started to look for a job. Three months later I was one more office worker riding a subway car uptown every morning, blending into a city of seven million.

Amado published his novel and got a few solid reviews, mostly by the few literary friends he had not alienated or offended over the years. Nothing was more valued in that tribe, where a diminutive amount of money and prestige had to be split among hundreds of starving wolves, than reputation. So even if they were his friends, they would have not dared praise his book in public if they thought it was awful. The praise, then, was genuine, and deserved. Rodrigo's agency made Amado a website to market and sell the novel, which I found online. It looked shoddy, but functional. I never figured out how the book did in the end. I even saw it once or twice at a bookstore near my apartment; it had been placed mistakenly on the Chilean Poetry shelf, in between Gonzalo Millán and Carmen Marai, but on the third occasion I passed by that shelf, looking for Julieta's latest book, it had vanished. The store's manager, who I chatted with occasionally, was surprised I had noticed the specimen and told me the four copies she had in stock had been unceremoniously called back by the publisher: a signal it wasn't selling well at all.

I got a signed copy in the post a few weeks later. It was dedicated to "the best editor in the world," and I realized that, both in the copyright page and in the colophon, I was credited as "lead editor." In a short hand-written letter, Amado told me excitedly that he was writing a novel about an alien invasion and said he hoped we might work together again in the future.

I hid his book under my pillow, and wondered why dreams seemed to become a reality both too soon and too late to make any kind of difference.
