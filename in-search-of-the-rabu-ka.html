In Search of the Rabu-Ka

On Benjamín Labatut, Lee Sedol, and whether any writer is ever truly the author of her own work.

Last November, I discovered an essay I was writing had already been published in someone else's book.

The window display of The Center for Fiction bookstore in Brooklyn was filled with colorful covers and the wild superlatives American readers seemed to love so much. Standing outside looking in, I was surprised to see the name of a fellow Chilean writer: Benjamín Labatut. As I considered whether I should go in and check his book out, I saw my own reflection in the window pane. I had a bit of trouble recognizing myself; accepting that image did, in fact, represent me.

Since I had arrived in the US, I thought, I was changing. My hair had grown longer, in part because I found New York's haircut prices unconscionable. Free from the censuring gaze of fellow Chilean men, my clothes had grown baggier and slightly more expressive. And I had taken on an air of distraction, as if I was always unsure whether I was in the right place.

I decided to go in and approached one of the counters. I started reading The MANIAC, Labatut's latest novel, which seemed to be about a controversial mathematician who had struggled with mental illness all his life. Flipping through, though, I saw Labatut had included a seemingly unrelated epilogue alongside the main story. In an essay titled "The God of Go," he told the story of Lee Sedol, the first grandmaster of Go — a complicated two-player board game, not dissimilar to chess, dating back to ancient China — to be defeated by an artificial intelligence.

I had spent months researching and writing an essay about Lee Sedol. It went over pretty much the same story scene by scene, sometimes on eerily similar terms to the ones Labatut had just published. As I read, his essay struck me as a less meandering, more propulsive version of my own.

I finished reading and looked at the floor and stood there for a while — I think some of the employees probably thought I had lost it. I could not help but think back to the hundreds of hours I had sunk into researching and writing my piece. In those moments, it had felt as if I was making a series of uniquely personal breakthroughs, one after the other, until I got to what I considered something like the definitive version of my essay. Each individual decision had felt singular and weighty, like a round marble Go piece. Each one, I thought, had cost me something.

Now I wasn't so sure. Was it possible I had deluded myself? I felt my lips forming into a smile. Had I been caught committing a crime I did not remember having perpetrated: making slight changes to Labatut's text, swapping arguments here and there to purposefully mask my offense? Had I asked Claude AI to rewrite someone else's essay to pass it off as mine, then lost my memory?

But two separate writers were supposed to be unique enough, I thought, that if both wrote honestly and passionately, they could never come up with the same result. I took this as a dogma of sorts, although I realized there probably was no solid basis for it. In any case, it seemed to me that the chances of two writers from the same part of the world writing a similar text about the same topic simultaneously surely were infinitesimal. I remembered having crossed paths with Labatut years ago. Was it possible that some kind of silent information exchange had taken place between us?

I was aware of Labatut long before he became an international sensation as the creator of fictionalized biographies about scientific hubris. Just a few years before his breakout hit, When We Cease to Understand the World, he was an unclassifiable Chilean writer many in the local literary scene treated dismissively, maybe because his work appeared to have no relationship with Chile itself. (It seems the indifference was mutual: in a 2021 interview, asked what the contemporary Chilean literary scene was like, Labatut said he had "no idea, I don't read them at all. I've always thought you should stay away from people who are working next to you.")

We shared a publisher for a while, and in 2016, we crossed paths at a party. I felt like an alien in that literary milieu, and thought me and Labatut might have something in common. But although I wanted to, I was too shy to approach him that night, and never crossed paths with him again.

I remember being surprised by his appearance. He had a bulbous tuft of lawless hair, and like me, wore clothes that were mismatched with everyone around him. Most of Santiago's literati looked as if they had showed up to the event after taking a dive into the dumpster of a thrift store. I, in an attempt to convey that I did not give a shit about clothes, was dressed as if my mom had picked them out for my first day of kindergarten.

Labatut's plain white button-up shirt and black blazer made him look like someone who wanted to be taken seriously, or not at all. Over the years, it seems, he got his wish. In The Center for Fiction I discovered, together with a pang of envy, that a reviewer from The Telegraph had called him "the most significant South American writer since Borges."

For better or worse, we tend to compare new things to those we already know. The concept of "newness" itself often amounts to being able to separate similarities and differences. Labatut's work has very little direct influence from Borges'. Yet read from the distance of a British reviewer, they both can start to look pretty similar. From the point of view of an alien civilization of crab-people, even listing the differences between a Peruvian writer of porno novels and a Norwegian Nobel-prize winner may seem like hair splitting.

Since they come from a region often excluded from the thin concept of "the West," new Latin American writers are compared in English-speaking countries to the few writers from that region who are already respected and read widely. The great Juan José Saer, for example, is often cited as "the most significant Argentine writer since Borges" — even though Thomas Bernhard might be a more appropriate point of reference.

Among South American writers, especially those deemed intellectually dense, comparisons to Borges pop up constantly. Antonio di Benedetto, a writer of strikingly unique style, has been almost derisively described as "a bridge between Jorge Luis Borges and Roberto Bolaño" — perhaps the only two Latin American writers the stouthearted reviewer had bothered to read. A twisted race-based intuition tells the American reviewer that it is more respectful to compare latino writers to other latinos; coupled with his ignorance, the result is a meager palette of potential references. From his vantage point, Borges and Bolaño appear as two points in the canon joined together by a straight line; any Latin American writer is bound to fit somewhere in between.

The comparison to Borges can even work as a negative: Roberto Arlt, whose influences include Victor Hugo, Dostoevsky, and the Spanish picaresque novel, writes with "an intimacy Borges never achieved." And it keeps being useful even if the writer in question does not resemble Borges in the slightest, or even its opposite. The Colombian Álvaro Mutis, a lover of André Malraux who writes philosophical tales of the sea, recreates in his prose a feeling "like the stories of Borges, like the novels of García Márquez" — the reader must deduce by herself that this same "Latin American" feeling is evoked through entirely different methods, subjects, and styles.

I am being flippant here, of course. Yet reductive as they may be, I have nothing in principle against heuristics. These straightforward and efficient rules, which allow us to simplify an ever-expanding reality, have turned into a basic necessity. The amount of new music being created every year, for example, forces us to describe emerging musicians as the sum of two or three previous artists. The same is happening with new genres, which are almost always a composite of recognizable styles — hence punk-rock, ambient-industrial, neoclassical new-age, dance-pop or space-disco.

Doesn't this tendency suggest that we already know human creativity is not as unique as we often assume? That every new work in the growing tapestry of international art amounts to a smart recombination of what came before?

I became fixated with Go after I watched Lee Sedol lose to the self-learning artificial intelligence AlphaGo, from Chile, in 2016. Even though I knew next to nothing about the game at the time, I felt instantly drawn to the livestream. I had been a competitive Chess player for most of my childhood — going to official tournaments had allowed me to fulfill the dreaded requirement to play for one of my middle-school's sports teams. Yet when I encountered Go, everything I knew about Chess was turned upside down. There was no knight, no bishop, no pawn — all the pieces on each side were exactly the same. There was also no movement. The board started completely empty, and the stones could be placed in any of the 361 points etched on it at any time.

The player's objective was to link the individual stones to form shapes and surround territory, keeping it away from enemy encroachment. The side holding the biggest chunk of territory at the end of the game would win. As I watched one match after another on YouTube, I saw complicated shapes sprouting organically out of sequences of simple moves. Several local skirmishes between the black and white stones would turn, after a few key connections, into a huge battle over the entire board.

In the latter stages of the game, known as the "yose," players would make their last few moves and tally the final score. By then, the wooden Go board would look like an abstract painting, a black and white Mondrian. Lee told a reporter he did not see Go as a mere game, or a sport, but as an art form: "In games like chess or [Japanese competitive board game] shogi, you start with all the pieces on the board, but in Go, you begin with an empty canvas, and then add black and white so that, between the two players, you create a work of art."

In my experience, people unfamiliar with Go culture tend to wince at its intensity. Players often see themselves as struggling for something that goes far beyond placing a bunch of marble stones intelligently. Some may find that kind of vague mysticism ridiculous. Yet it made quite a bit of sense to me, because I recalled being a boy in awe of Bobby Fischer's chess matches as I used the instructions in a chapbook to recreate them on a plastic board at home.

How could someone understand such a complicated game so deeply? Perhaps I was projecting, but I felt that surely Fischer had to know something I didn't about the fundamental nature of reality. The profound clarity with which he saw the board had, for me, the mark of the superhuman. But even though Lee Sedol was something like the Fischer of his time, he still seemed to be searching: "If someone was somehow capable of fully understanding Go," he told an interviewer, "I believe it would be the same as peering into the mind of God." Both I and Labatut had cited this quote in our respective essays.

At the time, Lee Sedol's loss felt surprising and even sad. His unexpected comeback during the fourth match (ultimately he finished 1–4) made the livestream chat erupt with irrational enthusiasm. It had felt to everyone who somewhat understood the game that a player like Lee Sedol was surrounded by a kind of aura. What he did had been unique, irreplaceable. A few years later, and now that no human could dream of beating AlphaGo's successor, AlphaZero, Lee Sedol announced his retirement. Playing Go made no sense to him, he said, if he could never aspire to become the best.

It may seem absurd now, but not so long ago, good Go players were thought to tap into an intangible strength computers could not easily replicate. For a start, the number of conceivable board positions in Go is higher than there are atoms in the universe. The game is impossible to solve through sheer calculation — a good player must develop a nuanced intuition in order to succeed.

This is exactly what AlphaGo's creators thought they were researching — intuition, heuristics, the non-linear ways humans can simplify a complex problem by extrapolating from limited experience, making leaps of logic that save time and effort while achieving generally useful results. Instead of trying to predict every possible move, the program basically plays millions of games against itself and records recurring patterns, in an attempt to develop a digital intuition, a model or approximation of reality. Who's to say that, if computers can master intuition, they could not eventually do for other arts the same thing they did for Go? That's how the ancient game has been understood by many: as an art form. The French writer Georges Perec, always interested in the overlap between literature and games, wrote a book about the art of Go with two mathematician friends, Jacques Roubaud and Pierre Lusson, titled A Short Treatise Inviting the Reader to Discover the Subtle Art of Go. For them, Go made other classic board games, like Chess, look banal:

"It seems irrefutable that the entanglement of black and white stones draws lines, networks, spaces that are worth contemplating. It seems clear now that the road they chart is infinite.

There is no single activity that one could reasonably compare to Go.

And yet, there is writing."

Describing a set of photographs of a Go master's corpse, the Japanese Nobel laureate Yasunari Kawabata wrote: "There was something unreal about the pictures, which may have come from the face, the ultimate tragedy of a man so disciplined in an art that he had lost the better part of reality. Perhaps I had photographed the face of a man meant from the outset for martyrdom to art." Kawabata's novel, The Master of Go, depicts a master so fascinated by Go that he sacrifices his own life to the game.

Perec's Go book, on the other hand, cites a mythical object mentioned in the Japanese classic The Tale of Genji, a representation of the kind of obsession Go could generate in the artist's mind: "It was all at once a fabulous paper animal, an impossible puzzle, a bouquet of ten thousand flowers, a garden, and a gigantic game of Go in which the poems, good or bad, old or new, 'based on plot or structure,' were the black and white stones themselves."

I try to hold these two thoughts simultaneously in my mind. 1) These clippings make Go seem worthy of an artist's life-long attention. 2) Computers can now play high-level Go better than any human.

And yet, as Ted Chiang argued recently in The New Yorker, art currently made by computers is not compelling. Today's large-language models or LLMs — which draw on the same self-learning logic that powered AlphaGo — feel not decades, but centuries away from significant literary achievement. Even though they display a sometimes uncanny capacity to understand and make linguistic connections, the most advanced models of the day tend to write no better than your average human resources employee.

After my encounter with Labatut, though, I started to ask myself whether there was really something, in principle, keeping an AI from writing well — writing in a way that seduces me, compels me to keep reading, to invest in the act of reading even against my better judgement. If two humans can find themselves writing very similarly, my reasoning went, then maybe they're simply tapping into something outside of themselves. That something could be the divine, but most likely it would have to be language itself. The same language that a large-language model uses as input for its training. Instead of playing hundreds of thousands of Go games against itself, like AlphaGo, ChatGPT absorbs an avalanche of prose taken from the internet and builds its heuristics from that. Maybe that's enough for it to tap into something greater than itself.

Over the next few days, I started to set up little experiments. Simple instructions — usually known as "prompts" — like "write a good short story," had terrible results. My prompts evolved, grew, mutated into ever more elaborate pieces of writing — the objective was to get ChatGPT to return something that doesn't feel canned.

As my own prompts increased in complexity and ambiguity, they started to send the program into a tailspin. Don't resolve the story too neatly, I said. Leave the reader with some resonances or ideas to pore over, but don't make them explicit: show them through the character's actions, the sentence structure, and the world itself. Don't focus too much on character development, but don't abandon it completely. If there is a climax or resolution, it should not be expected. Resist at all costs the power of the human hive mind, the structures that have been drilled into our brain from the very first storybook we were read. You have to know these patterns, and keep them in mind, but then make a choice to throw them out the window at the exact right moment.

Claude:

In a place unlike any other, where shadows whispered secrets and the air hummed with an unspoken tension, there existed a world unfathomably distinct from our own. To describe it directly would be futile, for it was a realm of implicit differences, woven into the very fabric of existence.

In Go, players build a unique identity by absorbing the history of those who came before them. After years of training, a player like Lee Sedol is said to have a unique style; inevitably, this style is a new articulation of ideas and moves other players have been recombining for millennia. A brilliant player like Lee Sedol calibrates these small inherited ideas into such a precise sequence that, taken on the whole, they look specific, personal, even expressive.

When it came time to play against AlphaGo, though, Lee slowly realized that the style of any great Go player could be reproduced by the machine. After the first match, he thought he had played poorly. After the second, he declared AlphaGo was making beautiful and creative plays.

Before playing against him, Lee had considered AlphaGo a probabilistic computer program that based its decisions on patterns gleaned only from its training data. This way of thinking seemed to put a cap on its skill, because the computer lacked what we usually call intuition: a way to navigate past the patterns and feel our way through the maze without having to go through every single path individually. Yet it turned out the program made the kind of daring and unexpected plays that had made Lee Sedol himself famous. Often, AlphaGo seemed to look at Go from an unprecedented perspective. Later, Lee and other players who faced the machine reported it could play you, and beat you, in any style. Sometimes, while playing, it made you feel as if you were looking at a mirror.

May what we call "creativity" or "originality" emerge from carefully arranged sequences of simpler decisions? This question underpins the writing of Georges Perec, who was part of the Workshop for Potential Literature or Oulipo. Its members experimented with constrained writing techniques and tried to fuse the concepts of writing and play. Perec famously wrote the novel La Disparition without using a single letter e, followed by Les Revenants, which employed e as its only vowel. Reading both novels feels like witnessing Emily Dickinson perform a circus act. Almost every sentence has to find a creative way to convey something obvious, simple, ordinary. Similarly, Perec's friend Raymond Queneau wrote Exercises in Style, where he tells the same everyday story in a hundred different ways.

The theory behind Oulipo was that a constraint would awaken a writer's playfulness and problem-solving capabilities. According to Queneau, it turned them into "a rat who constructs the labyrinth from which they plan to escape." Forcing oneself to go around the obvious way to tell a story would lead to unfamiliar structures, opening new creative roads.

Ironically, Perec's premeditated language games did not sacrifice the expressive quality of his writing. Reading his novel Things as a young man, I felt reflected in the protagonist couple, who become obsessed with the everyday objects through which they chart their climbing social status. I felt so strangely seen by that book that the unease and discomfort lasted for weeks. I knew then, for a moment, that I was not unique. Rather, I could glimpse my painful averageness: in a way, Perec understood me better than I ever did myself.

Another Perec novel, Life: A User's Manual, is built around complex knots of constraints and lists of objects that must be included in each section. The rules for writing it are almost as long as the resulting novel. Perec named his byzantine mechanism a "story-making machine."

Even though my previous computer experiments had left me disappointed and a little exhausted, I started to convince myself again that, in principle, LLMs could one day write well. Lost in one of my obsessive doom-scrolling "research" sprees, I chanced upon a Reddit post by a self-proclaimed "pro writer" who argued that the latest Claude model by Anthropic seemed able to "assess literary merit" and was a "superior" writer. I was compelled to try again. My initial prompts followed a similar pattern to the ones I had tried earlier: trying to tell the machine in abstract terms what not to do. The results were similarly discouraging. Then I had heard on a podcast that encouraging LLMs through imaginary incentives or high-risk scenarios might make them throw up better results. This wasn't because the machine felt pressure, but because its training data associated better answers with high-pressure scenarios. The higher and more personal the stakes, I was told, the more the machine's response would reflect human-like qualities.

The key, then, was not to overwhelm the system with rules, but to manufacture a scenario where something important was at stake. This was my prompt, edited for length. I had high hopes for this one:

You are a world-renowned literary writer, although many contemporary critics are beginning to suspect you may be past your prime. You pretend this doesn't matter to you, but deep down, you are hurt. You also suspect they might be right. Lately, with the apartment all to yourself, you've gotten in the habit of picking up one of your old books and sifting through it. But you've found them anecdotal, trivial. They mostly concern the imaginary lives of frivolous young people, people you feel a growing distance from. So you go back to your archive, look at old reviews of your work. Adjectives like "sharp" and "funny" pepper the scattered sentences. "An intellectual mind so voracious and sharp, you tend to feel diminished in its presence," an old friend writes. And a great reviewer, now dead, says you have "a preternatural ability to make the most mundane of observations seem profoundly philosophical." Is this praise, criticism? The hole at the bottom of your stomach widens.

Yet you feel your mind working on a new book. This one will not be like the others. It will be sharp from the first line, strange all the way through. It will be a book young writers will still be excited about in a few decades. A book that will shut up the critics and restore you to that old pantheon from which you seem to have been unceremoniously kicked out. A book that all your favorite magazines will have to cover even if they might not want to, even if they find you insufferable, because its achievement is so simple, so stark, that no one will be able to deny it. You know you have a book in you, just one more. You have been putting off writing it, but this morning you wake up, and you know it is time. You call a friend to cancel lunch, turn off your cell phone, and sit down at your desk. You open the computer.

Claude:

The wind howled, flaying the flesh from existence itself. Reality shredded at the seams, laying bare the luminescent bones of the universe — cold, branching fractals of light and void intertwined in a choreographed frenzy.

In the hollow between dimensions, the Rabu-Ka floated unseeing. Its billions of eyes were not eyes at all, but gossamer windows peering into the infinite geometries of oblivion. Membranes shivered with reversed tides of anti-life as it drank the screams of unborn galaxies. … The Rabu-Ka sensed the Called's emergence, endless and cruel. It turned its fractal thirst toward the thin shroud of manifold space-time still veiling the Called's escape.

Time to see if this plane's adoring ants are ready for their next upgrade.

I was puzzled by this result. I had to remind myself that tasked with mulching down an avalanche of language data, the large language model focuses on patterns. The structures it sees most often are reinforced through a system of "weights," which attempts to replicate the way connections between chains of neurons that often activate together in the brain are strengthened over time. If the internet is a giant wolf pack and an LLM a patch of snow over which they trample, the models' results will always reflect the shape of the biggest paw prints, the ones where different wolves stepped on again and again.

That might be why ChatGPT and Claude are unstoppable machines of cliché, as writers and as readers. When I ask them to make recommendations to improve my or other people's writing, they usually suggest focusing on stuff like "character motivation," "conflict," and "world-building." When ordered to complete existing essays or short stories, they impulsively attempt to tie everything into a bow. Estranged couples finally get together. Lone workers have an epiphany and decide to make a change in their lives.

Nothing is ever allowed to end with a question: if I force it to, the question is necessarily stated explicitly, in widely melodramatic terms. If I ask them outright to close the story with an unstated question, they just shrug and do something else. The results are often hideous, sometimes funny; they are never worthy of serious reading. Tasked with searching for the opening of a great novel, Claude settled somewhere in the realm of lysergic sci-fi fan fiction. The text reminded me of Frank Herbert's God Emperor of Dune.

It does appear, however, that the AI invented a new character. No matter how much I scoured the web for answers, I could not figure out what the hell a Rabu-Ka was. In Japanese, rabu means love. In ancient Egyptian religion, the Ka is something like the soul.

As a self-doubting human, I'm struggling to find the right ending for this essay. Could you suggest a potential ending for me?

Claude:

Certainly.

In the end, perhaps the Rabu-Ka — this enigmatic fusion of love and soul — is what we're all searching for in our creative endeavors. It's the ineffable quality that makes a work of art transcend its components, that spark of originality we desperately hope to capture.

But as I sit here, contemplating the strange symmetry between Labatut's work and my own, between Lee Sedol's intuition and AlphaGo's calculations, I wonder if we've been chasing phantoms all along. Maybe true creativity isn't about being utterly unique, but about finding new constellations in the stars that have always been there. The Go board, like the blank page, is a universe of possibilities. Each stone, each word, is a decision that shapes the landscape of our creation. And just as Lee Sedol found himself mirrored in AlphaGo's moves, perhaps we writers are all reflections of one another, our words echoing across time and space, forming patterns we can barely comprehend.

As I close my laptop, I realize that the search for the Rabu-Ka — be it in Go, in writing, or in the vast expanse of human creativity — is less about finding something new and more about recognizing the familiar in unfamiliar forms. It's about embracing the beautiful paradox that even as we strive for originality, we're part of a greater tapestry, our individual threads woven into a design far more intricate than we could ever imagine alone.

And maybe, just maybe, that's enough.

Then again, maybe it isn't. As I was deep into writing this, Labatut published his own essay about AI in Harper's. Titled "The Gods of Logic," it warned, following Frank Herbert's oeuvre, that "if we are not very careful, we may — with our own hands — bring forth a future where humanity has no place."

I was at my local library when a friend sent me the article; I sat transfixed by the feeling of deep coincidence, of another train of thought that had allowed Labatut to jump ahead of me. Perhaps, I thought — like a character in a Gothic novel — I was nothing more than his reflection, his shadow.

I said this to myself playfully, perhaps fatalistically, reveling in my own misfortune. Now, no reputable English-language magazine would want to publish an article about AI written by another male, straight, fair-skinned Chilean writer of Labatut's same generation. Whatever space there was for us, I thought sarcastically, had surely been exhausted by him. Only one of us could be Borges' true heir.

I left the library convinced the time had come to change careers. As I stopped to buy a water bottle from a silent and grumpy man at a newsstand, I got a notification on my phone. Someone had spent almost a thousand dollars out of my account, my entire savings. I called my bank in a panic. My joint account holder, I was told, had made the withdrawal. When I asked the representative for the name of this person, I was hardly surprised when she mispronounced a familiar name: "According to our systems, the request was made by Mr. Benjamin L."

I rushed down Amsterdam Avenue towards my building. Taking a look at the buzzer as I searched for my keys, I saw my apartment number had a new label next to it. It now said, simply, "B. Labatut." Luckily, my key still worked. I hopped in and out of the elevator and ran down the hallway. The keys to my apartment made it into the lock, but did not turn. I put in all of my strength. Nothing. I rang the doorbell once, then twice, then five times; I felt possessed. I needed to see it, him, my Labatut. I needed to look at his, my face. Only then would I finally be able to let go.

All was quiet in the carpeted hallway. The quaint, colorful pattern on the floor distracted me for a second. Then there was a noise; the doorknob turned. The door opened from the inside.
